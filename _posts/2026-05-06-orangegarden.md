#### A Historical Overview
The legacy of Orange Garden started in 1927, with Chan Woot as its founder. In 1933, George Chen bought it from Woot. After Chen's retirement in the 70s, a man named Charn Yuen took over. Finally, in 2008, Orange Garden was bought by Hui Ruan, and his family has been running it ever since. Hui Ruan and George Chen are both from the same Canton region in China: Taishan. It is where a lot of early (pre-1960) Chinese immigrants came from. Taishan is a part of the Guangdong province on the South China Sea. Many immigrants came from this area because it is a port city and trade hub.  

Chop Suey is originally noted in the 1880s as a Chinese American dish, not traditional Chinese. Because of the Chinese-Exclusion Act, Chinese people pre-1960 were generally not able to immigrate to the United States. This caused a development of a new 'culture' where Chinese Americans assimilated into what white Americans thought their culture was. Chinese restaurants had to adapt their menu for both Chinese and non-Chinese populations, as they could not survive with just one source of customers.  

#### Geography and Neighborhood
This Sanborn map is from 1905. Most of the buildings were made of wood, while a couple were made of bricks. North Center was a large brick manufacturer, and as a result, a lot of the buildings were made of it. This street corner seems to be somewhat of a material  outlier. In terms of businesses, Orange Garden's building was not made yet. However, the street remains a busy business center. It contains a bowling alley, a paint store, a tailor, and other community hubs, making it the perfect location for a new restaurant.

#### Family and Community
While the original owner of Orange Garden is speculated, it is believed to be Chan Woot. He was born in 1891 and lived in 1942 W. Irving Park Road (the Orange Garden location). He was born in China, and his occupation is listed as “restaurant.” He is listed as “W” (or white) in the census because they didn't denote races other than Black or white.

This document is from the 1950 American Census. George Chen is listed at apartment #3935 Keystone Ave. Keystone is a residential street running North-South that is South of Irving Park Road and East of Pulaski. The house is in the Irving Park neighborhood. He was the head of house and had 5 kids and a wife. He was listed in the restaurant business and owned his own business. Chen was born in China in 1906. The street the Chen’s lived on had a much higher population of Chinese people than Chan Woot's. However, that could just be because this census taker wrote down that they were Chinese, while Woot’s census taker wrote he was white.

#### The Business and Commercial History
The current and former North Center community members rave about how delicious their food was and how they used to go all the time when they were kids on websites like Yelp and Facebook. The restaurant has such a reach, that they've shipped food as far as New York for their homesick customers. Even before Orange Garden and surrounding businesses, the corner of Damen and Irving was a commercial hub.

This mural has been hanging inside Orange Garden since its composition circa 1935. The artist came to Chicago for the Century of Progress World’s Fair and painted murals for Chen’s other restaurants as well.

This iconic Chop Suey sign, commissioned circa 1961, was put on the market in 2022. Hui Ruan's children didn't want their father to be so engrossed in the business, so they were planning to sell. However, Ben Ruan (the son of Hui) has since taken over the restaurant. The sign was sold to Madame Zuzu's, a cafe in Highland Park owned by The Smashing Pumpkins frontman Billy Corgan. Corgan promised to preserve the sign and its importance to the history of Chicago.
