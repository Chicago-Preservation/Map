#### Introduction

Douglas is the northernmost community area within Bronzeville, bounded by 26th Street to the north, Pershing Road to the south, the Dan Ryan Expressway to the west, and Lake Michigan to the east. It takes its name from Stephen A. Douglas, Abraham Lincoln's political rival and 1860 presidential opponent. The area served as a Civil War training and prison camp (Camp Douglas) before becoming home to affluent white Chicagoans — including a large Jewish population — drawn by the Grand Boulevard, now Martin Luther King Drive, designed by Frederick Law Olmsted in the 1870s as one of Chicago's first suburban-style tree-lined carriage drives.

Demographic change accelerated in the early twentieth century as the Great Migration brought Black families from the South. Chicago's industrial surplus of steel and meatpacking jobs and the advocacy of the *Chicago Defender* made Bronzeville a primary destination. Racially restrictive housing policies confined Black residents to the "Black Belt," and Douglas became part of what residents called the Black Metropolis — reaching peak population from the 1910s through the 1940s and remaining influential through the 1960s.

Mid-century decline came through multiple channels: the lifting of housing restrictions dispersed the middle class; "urban renewal" by the Chicago Housing Authority displaced thousands; and economic disinvestment accelerated demolition. In the 1990s, renewed interest — particularly among Black professionals and intellectuals — brought preservation efforts back to Douglas. Six buildings on this tour hold Chicago Landmark status, but as this tour shows, that designation does not protect structure, use, or heritage in any uniform way.

#### Tour Stops

**1. Unity Hall**
*3140 S Indiana Ave*

![Unity Hall]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_01_unity_hall.png)

Designed by local architect L.B. Dixon in red brick with terra cotta decorations and sheet metal edging — a characteristic example of 1880s Chicago commercial architecture. Built in 1887 as the Lakeside Club, a Jewish social club, the building passed to Black political use in 1917 when Alderman Oscar Stanton De Priest founded the Peoples Movement Club and made it his headquarters. De Priest was the first Black person to serve on the Chicago City Council and, elected in 1928, the first Black U.S. Representative from a northern state. His successor, William L. Dawson, who served in the Chicago House of Representatives for twenty-seven years, later used it as his political headquarters. From the 1950s onward the building served various churches. It is currently vacant, listed on Preservation Chicago's most endangered properties since 2012, and awaiting resources for restoration.

---

**2. Lincoln Perry Apartments and Annex**
*3245 S Prairie Ave / 243 E 32nd St*

![Lincoln Perry Apartments]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_02_lincoln_perry.png)

This nine-story, 183-unit senior housing complex, constructed in 1973 by the Chicago Housing Authority, is named after Lincoln Theodore Monroe Andrew Perry — better known by his stage name Stepin Fetchit. Perry was a vaudeville artist, comedian, and the first Black actor to receive screen credit in a film. He lived in these apartments in the 1970s, and his neighbors petitioned the CHA to name the building after him. The stop opens a theme the tour returns to: preservation through naming rather than physical structure. Giving the names of Black historical figures to spaces occupied by majority Black populations is an intentional act of keeping those figures in collective memory.

---

**3. Pilgrim Baptist Church**
*3301 S Indiana Ave*

![Pilgrim Baptist Church]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_03_pilgrim_baptist.png)

Originally the Kehilath Anshe Ma'ariv Synagogue (1890), built for Chicago's oldest Jewish congregation and designed by Louis Sullivan and Dankmar Adler. Sullivan coined "Form ever follows function," though this early work still carries Romanesque Revival features — heavy rough-cut stone walls, deeply inset windows, and arched doorways — predating the purely functionalist turn. The Jewish congregation moved out in 1922; Pilgrim Baptist Church took over, and under choir director Thomas A. Dorsey — widely regarded as the father of Gospel music — the building became the birthplace of Gospel. Mahalia Jackson, Albertina Walker, and James Cleveland all performed here.

A 2006 fire, started during a roof renovation funded entirely by church collections, destroyed everything except the external walls, including decades of historical records and Dorsey's original sheet music. The congregation now meets across the street. A plan for a National Museum for Gospel Music was formulated in 2017 but has been repeatedly delayed by the 2020 Midwest Derecho (which knocked out the south wall), the COVID-19 pandemic, and persistent funding gaps. By 2024, approximately $7 million in combined state and grant funding had been secured, against an estimated $10 million needed for exterior wall repair alone.

---

**4. Illinois College of Optometry**
*3241 S Michigan Ave*

![Illinois College of Optometry]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_04_illinois_college_optometry.png)

The largest optometry school in the U.S., founded in 1955 through a merger of two Chicago optometry colleges. Its glass-and-steel building is inspired by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe, whose Crown Hall at IIT stands two blocks west. Both buildings — constructed in the 1950s, a period of severe disinvestment in Douglas — share clean lines, large glass expanses, and absence of ornamentation. The comparison with Pilgrim Baptist directly across the street is the point: when the 2006 fire blew out ICO windows, repairs were completed without difficulty or headlines. Twenty years later, the church across the street remains in ruins. The contrast reflects a long-standing pattern of skewed investment priorities in historically Black and lower-income neighborhoods.

---

**5. Chicago Defender Building**
*3435 S Indiana Ave*

![Chicago Defender Building]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_05_chicago_defender.png)

Built in 1899 by Henry Newhouse as a synagogue for the South Side Hebrew Congregation, purchased by the *Chicago Defender* in 1920 — again marking the 1920s–30s pattern of Jewish institutional spaces transitioning to Black community use as Jewish residents moved north. The *Defender*, founded in 1905 by Robert S. Abbott, was the most widely read Black newspaper of its era, known for its outspoken civil rights editorials and for promoting the Great Migration. Black Pullman Porters distributed it along their southern routes. The *Defender* occupied the building until 1960. It is a Chicago Landmark and its facade is well maintained — but the building now houses a medical supply store. This raises the tour's central question: what is preserved when structure survives but use changes entirely? Is the Pilgrim Baptist congregation's commitment to keeping its ruin connected to Gospel history more authentically preservationist than this?

**Interlude: 35th Street Corridor**
*E 35th St from S Indiana Ave to S Giles Ave*

At the turn of the century this stretch was entirely storefronts. Today a McDonald's and a Payless Shoes occupy much of what were independent commercial buildings, with parking lots breaking up the original density — yet the street still functions as a commercial corridor in recognizable ways. Two corner buildings at 234 and 235 E 35th St retain their original chamfered facades and brick corner posts — a characteristic feature of turn-of-the-century Chicago tied houses (saloons exclusively selling a single brewer's product, most commonly Schlitz). The chamfered corners widened sidewalk space and encouraged gathering. In 1912 both were saloons; today one is an Indian grocery and the other is vacant.

---

**6. Eighth Regiment Armory**
*3533 S Giles Ave*

![Eighth Regiment Armory]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_06_eighth_regiment_armory.png)

Built in 1914 by J.B. Dibelka, this fortress-like red brick and limestone structure was the first armory in the United States constructed for a Black military regiment. The Eighth Regiment — the "Fighting 8th" — traced its roots to an 1871 volunteer militia. Incorporated into the Illinois National Guard during the Spanish-American War of 1898, its soldiers formed the first all-Black officer corps in American military history. In WWI, incorporated into the 370th Infantry alongside the French army, they were nicknamed "Black Devils" by German forces. The armory closed in the 1960s, reopened as the South-Central Gymnasium until 1999, and was then extensively renovated as Chicago Military Academy, a public high school. The exterior was preserved and an expansion designed to match the original Romanesque Revival facade was added along 35th Street. Though the interior is entirely different, the physical structure and its continued military-educational use carry the history of the Fighting 8th forward in a legible way.

**7. Formerly Sunset Café**
*315 E 35th St*

![Formerly Sunset Café]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_07_sunset_cafe.png)

Built in 1909 as an automobile garage and remodeled into the Sunset Café in 1921, this was one of Chicago's earliest and most consequential jazz venues. Louis Armstrong, Johnny Dodds, and Earl Hines played in the house band; Cab Calloway, Charlie Parker, Nat King Cole, Billie Holiday, and Dizzy Gillespie performed regularly; in 1939 Ella Fitzgerald made her Chicago debut on this stage. The Sunset Café was a "Black and Tan" club — racially integrated at a time of intense segregation. Remodeled and renamed the Grand Terrace Café in 1937, it operated as a beloved nightclub until 1950. It became an Ace Hardware store in the 1970s. Recently, Amazon purchased the building and converted it to the Amazon Urban Beautique, removing the glass brick windows, repainting the upper story, and replacing wooden paneling with brick. The building is now unrecognizable as what it was. It has held Chicago Landmark status since 1998 — but that designation offers no protection against alteration. Six buildings on this tour are Chicago Landmarks; Unity Hall sits vacant and deteriorating while this one has been stripped of its historic features entirely.

---

**8. Ida B. Wells Preparatory Elementary School**
*249 E 37th St*

![Ida B. Wells Preparatory Elementary School]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_08_ida_b_wells_school.png)

Constructed in 1961 to serve the Douglas community, this school is included not for architectural significance but for the power of its name. Ida B. Wells — journalist, sociologist, educator, activist, and NAACP co-founder — lived in Douglas and appears at the tour's final stop. Naming a school after her, the tour argues, may preserve her legacy more durably than preserving her house: her name lives in the vernacular memory of every child who passes through, rather than in the stops of a walking tour.

---

**9. Hammer/Palmer Mansion**
*3654–3656 S Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. Drive*

![Hammer/Palmer Mansion]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_09_hammer_palmer_mansion.png)

Built 1886–1888 by William Wilson Clay in Queen Anne style for Justice D. Harry Hammer — alderman, attorney, and Cook County Judge. In 1976 it became the home of Lu F. Palmer, Black activist, writer, radio host, and newspaper publisher. Palmer wrote for the *Chicago Defender* and was a pivotal supporter of Harold Washington's election as Chicago's first Black mayor. The mansion fell into disrepair in recent decades and faced demolition until it was purchased by the Obsidian Collective Archive (OCA), a nonprofit dedicated to preserving Black historic and modern journalism through digital archiving. The OCA plans to convert it into a resource for journalists and creatives — a potential model for how other endangered Douglas properties, including Pilgrim Baptist, might find sustainable second lives.

---

**10. Ida B. Wells House**
*3624 S Martin Luther King Dr*

![Ida B. Wells House]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_10_ida_b_wells_house.png)

Built 1889–1890 by Joseph Thain in a combined Romanesque/Queen Anne style with rough-cut stone walls and a prominent bay window. Wells lived here from 1919 to 1930 — during which time she founded the Negro Fellowship League (a community center for Black men arriving during the Great Migration), the Alpha Suffrage Club (to engage Black women voters), and the first kindergarten for Black children in Chicago. The house is a National Historic Landmark. The tour notes critically that Wells moved often during her lifetime, and it is not obvious why this particular house was chosen — raising the broader question of how preservation selects which sites to memorialize and whether the "George Washington slept here" standard of landmark designation systematically excludes people of color and communities of color from recognition.

---

**11. Martin Roche–John Tait House**
*3614 S Martin Luther King Drive*

![Martin Roche–John Tait House]({{site.baseurl}}/images/gonsky_11_roche_tait_house.png)

Built in 1888 by Martin Roche — half of the prominent Chicago architectural firm Holabird & Roche — in Romanesque Revival style. The flat, rough-cut stone facade contrasts sharply with the highly ornamented Queen Anne houses that dominate this stretch of Martin Luther King Drive. Roche lived here with his sister and brother-in-law John Tait until 1917. Holabird & Roche designed some of Chicago's first skyscrapers, including the Tacoma Building (1889, demolished 1929) and the Marquette Building (1895). The house is discussed alongside the Wells House to make the comparison explicit: the Roche-Tait House was preserved purely because of the fame of its builder; the Wells House holds significance because of the activist work carried out within it. Both cases illustrate that the "value" attributed to historic structures is rarely intrinsic — it is assigned, and the assignment reflects whose history is deemed worth preserving.

---

#### Conclusion

Douglas has produced some of the nation's greatest thinkers, artists, and activists — and its built fabric, from the armory of the Fighting 8th to the birthplace of Gospel music to the homes of Ida B. Wells and Louis Armstrong, holds that history in tangible form. Yet the neighborhood has been continuously undervalued: landmark status has not protected Unity Hall from vacancy or Sunset Café from alteration; Pilgrim Baptist remains in ruins twenty years after its fire. A renewed preservation movement, led largely by Black professionals and institutions, is beginning to reverse this — the OCA's purchase of the Hammer/Palmer Mansion is one example — but the gap between the neighborhood's historical significance and the resources committed to its preservation remains wide.
