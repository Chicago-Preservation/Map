Douglas is the northernmost community area within Bronzeville, spanning from 26th Street to Pershing Road and from the Dan Ryan Expressway to Lake Michigan. Named after Senator Stephen A. Douglas — Abraham Lincoln's political rival — the neighborhood also served as a Civil War training and prison camp called Camp Douglas. In the late 19th century, Douglas was home to many affluent white Chicagoans drawn by the development of Grand Boulevard (now Martin Luther King Drive), one of Chicago's first suburban-style boulevards designed by Frederick Law Olmsted.

In the early 20th century, the Great Migration transformed Douglas into the heart of Chicago's "Black Metropolis." Discriminatory housing policies confined Black families to the Black Belt, concentrating the population and creating a self-sustaining enclave of theaters, music venues, and businesses. Jazz and gospel legends including Nat King Cole, Louis Armstrong, and Mahalia Jackson performed here. By the mid-20th century, lifting of racial housing restrictions and subsequent "urban renewal" projects by the Chicago Housing Authority displaced thousands of residents, and the neighborhood experienced severe disinvestment. Since the 1990s, renewed interest — particularly from Black professionals and intellectuals — has brought new preservation efforts and community investment. This tour explores that full arc.

## Stop 1: Unity Hall
**3140 S. Indiana Avenue**

Unity Hall is a fitting beginning to this tour. The red brick building, designed by local architect L.B. Dixon with terra cotta decorations and sheet metal edging, was built in 1887 as the Lakeside Club, a Jewish social club. In 1917, Chicago Alderman Oscar Stanton De Priest — the first Black person to serve on the Chicago City Council and the first Black U.S. Representative from the northern states — founded the Peoples Movement Club here. Later, congressman William L. Dawson used the building as his political headquarters. By the mid-20th century it served primarily as a church space. Today it is vacant and sits on Preservation Chicago's most-endangered properties list. Unity Hall encapsulates the full cycle of Douglas: from affluent white community institution, to Black political headquarters, to victim of disinvestment.

## Stop 2: Lincoln Perry Apartments
**3245 S. Prairie Avenue / 243 E. 32nd Street**

These two large buildings — a nine-story, 183-unit housing complex constructed in 1973 — are named for Lincoln Theodore Monroe Andrew Perry, better known by his stage name Stepin Fetchit. Perry was a vaudeville artist, comedian, and the first Black man to receive screen credit in a film. He was financially successful, wrote for the Chicago Defender, and lived in these apartments in the 1970s; his neighbors petitioned the Chicago Housing Authority to name the building in his honor. As Gonsky argues, preservation of memory doesn't require the preservation of a building — naming can carry history into the daily lives of a community, keeping historical figures alive in collective memory.

## Stop 3: Pilgrim Baptist Church
**3301 S. Indiana Avenue**

What stands here today are the ruins of what was once the Kehilath Anshe Ma'ariv Synagogue, built in 1890 for Chicago's oldest Jewish congregation by architects Louis Sullivan and Dankmar Adler. In 1922, the Jewish congregation moved out and Pilgrim Baptist Church moved in. The church's choir director, Thomas A. Dorsey, is widely regarded as the father of Gospel music, and Pilgrim Baptist is considered the birthplace of Gospel. Mahalia Jackson, Albertina Walker, and James Cleveland all performed here. In 2006, a fire destroyed everything except the external walls. A plan to convert the space into a National Museum for Gospel Music has faced persistent funding shortfalls; as of this writing, exterior wall repair alone is projected at $10 million. Twenty years after the fire, the ruins remain — a painful contrast to the intact Illinois College of Optometry directly across the street.

---

## Stop 4: Illinois College of Optometry
**3241 S. Michigan Avenue**

The Illinois College of Optometry (ICO), founded in 1955 as the largest optometry school in the U.S., occupies a glass-and-steel building inspired by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe — close in form to Mies's Crown Hall two blocks west. ICO's building was constructed at the height of Bronzeville's disinvestment, during the same period when Pilgrim Baptist was relying on community collections to fund its own repairs. When the 2006 fire struck Pilgrim Baptist, the blast blew out some ICO windows — a repair so minor it made no headlines. This stark contrast illustrates how preservation resources in historically Black and lower-income neighborhoods are deprioritized, while better-resourced institutions just across the street receive maintenance without friction.

## Stop 5: Chicago Defender Building
**3435 S. Indiana Avenue**

This building was constructed in 1899 by Henry Newhouse as a synagogue for the South Side Hebrew Congregation. In 1920, the Chicago Defender moved in — following the wave of former Jewish spaces that turned over to Black institutions as Jewish families moved north. The Defender, founded in 1905 by Robert S. Abbott, was the most widely read Black newspaper in the country, carried by Pullman Porters along their southern routes. Its advocacy for northern migration helped fuel the Great Migration itself. The building was the Defender's home from 1920 to 1960. Today its facade is intact and it is a Chicago Landmark — but it now operates as a medical supply store. The building is preserved; its history as a space of empowerment and activism is not.

---

## Stop 6: Eighth Regiment Armory
**3533 S. Giles Avenue**

This massive structure — the former Eighth Regiment Armory, built in 1914 by J.B. Dibelka — was the first armory in the United States built for a Black military regiment. The Fighting 8th traces its roots to a volunteer militia group in 1871 and made history as the first all-Black officer corps during the Spanish-American War of 1898. During WWI, incorporated into the 370th Infantry, they served with the French army with such ferocity that the Germans called them the "Black Devils." Closed in the 1960s and reopened as a gymnasium, the building was later extensively renovated and now serves as Chicago Military Academy, a public high school. The red brick facade, trimmed in limestone with wooden arched doorways, was preserved and the school's expansion was designed to match the original, carrying the history of the Fighting 8th forward in both structure and use.

## Stop 7: Formerly Sunset Café
**315 E. 35th Street**

You would not guess it from looking at it now, but this building — today an Amazon Urban Beautique beauty supply store — was one of the most important jazz venues in American history. Built in 1909 as an automobile garage and remodeled in 1921, the Sunset Café was a "Black and Tan" club where jazz legends Louis Armstrong, Johnny Dodds, and Earl Hines held the bandstand. Cab Calloway, Charlie Parker, Nat King Cole, Billie Holiday, Dizzy Gillespie, and Ella Fitzgerald — who made her Chicago debut here in 1939 — all performed at this address. In 1937 it was renamed the Grand Terrace Café, a beloved nightclub until 1950. The building has held Chicago Landmark status since 1998, but Amazon recently removed the glass brick windows, repainted the upper story, and replaced the wooden paneling with brick. The building is now unrecognizable as the place it once was.

## Stop 8: Ida B. Wells Preparatory Elementary School
**249 E. 37th Street**

This school, constructed in 1961 to serve the Douglas community, is included on this tour not for its architecture but for its name. Ida B. Wells was a journalist, sociologist, educator, writer, activist, and a founder of the NAACP. She lived in Douglas for a time, and her house is one of our final stops. Gonsky argues that attaching Wells's name to a school in active use may have greater preservation impact than maintaining her house: her legacy lives on in the vernacular memory of every student who passes through these doors.

---

## Stop 9: Hammer/Palmer Mansion
**3654–3656 S. Martin Luther King, Jr. Drive**

The Hammer/Palmer Mansion was built from 1886 to 1888 by William Wilson Clay in the Queen Anne style for Justice D. Harry Hammer. It passed to 'Lu' F. Palmer in 1976 — a Black activist, writer, reporter, radio host, and newspaper publisher who championed Harold Washington's election as Chicago's first Black mayor. In recent decades the building has fallen into disrepair and faces demolition threat. It was recently purchased by Obsidian Collective Archive (OCA), a nonprofit dedicated to preserving Black historic and modern journalism. The OCA hopes to convert the mansion into a resource for journalists and creatives — a project that could, if realized, also bring greater visibility to Douglas and its neighboring sites like Pilgrim Baptist.

---

## Stop 10: Ida B. Wells House
**3624 S. Martin Luther King Drive**

The Ida B. Wells House was built from 1889 to 1890 by Joseph Thain in a combined Romanesque/Queen Anne style. Wells lived here from 1919 to 1930 with her family after she founded the Negro Fellowship League, a community center for Black men arriving during the Great Migration. During her time here she also organized the Alpha Suffrage Club to engage Black women voters, and founded the first kindergarten for Black children. The house is a National Historic Landmark, and as Gonsky observes, that designation reflects a preservation culture that has historically ignored people of color — making its survival all the more significant.

---

## Stop 11: Martin Roche–John Tait House
**3614 S. Martin Luther King Drive**

Adjacent to the Wells House, the Roche-Tait House was built in 1888 in the Romanesque Revival style by Martin Roche — half of the prominent Chicago architectural firm Holabird & Roche, which designed some of the city's first skyscrapers. Its flat, rough-cut stone facade stands in stark contrast to the ornate Queen Anne houses around it. Roche lived here with his sister and her husband, John Tait, until 1917. Like the Wells House, its preservation owes entirely to the fame of its builder and resident. Together, these two houses close a tour that has traced how memory, naming, use, and care — more than landmark designation alone — determine what survives.

---

*Douglas's history is not a story of gradual decline but of cyclical upheaval and persistent self-determination. What remains here — the ruins of Pilgrim Baptist beside the intact ICO, the renamed school, the mansions at risk — reveals a landscape where preservation resources have long been unevenly distributed. The work of restoration is ongoing.*
