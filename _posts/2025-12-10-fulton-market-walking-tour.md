#### Introduction

With over 120 restaurants, hundreds of residential units, the nation's most prestigious hotels and gyms, the Fulton Market district is Chicago's "intersection of food, design, commerce, art and culture." Chicago's most up-and-coming neighborhood, Fulton Market is a compelling case study reflecting multiple layers of history. Its origins — demonstrating Chicago's earliest examples of commerce and economic activity — are entangled with the current reinvention of the neighborhood.

The Fulton Market District is particularly compelling to study because its present-day use maintains a clear connection to its industrial past, preserving the district's significance even as its functions have transformed. Located in the Near West Side, the district was first put to use around 1850 as a food market. It is the first and oldest food market in Chicago, used for wholesale food and meat distribution. It serves as a symbol of Chicago's importance as a hub for food commerce in the center of the Midwest.

![Workers in Fulton Market, c. 1950s]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_01_workers.png)
{:.image}
Workers in the Fulton Market wholesale food district, c. 1952. The men captured here are workers in the food markets, central to the area's livelihood and success. (Zimmerman, L.F., 1952, Chicago History Museum)
{:.caption}

The district has transformed from a swarming wholesale food center into one of Chicago's major culinary destinations, preserving its historic connection to food while reinventing its identity. However, this transformation also raises concerns: with rapid development, the area risks becoming a "theme park," its historical heritage advertised as an aesthetic to attract tourists and high-end consumers.

Historically, winners in the Fulton Market district were low-income workers sustaining their family-owned businesses and larger meatpacking and wholesale food distributors. It was accessible to lower-income working class individuals. Today, the district caters to rich corporate entities, developers, and high-income professionals. Small, family-owned businesses that are not financially backed by larger entities are no longer able to operate in the area, resulting in working class communities being displaced.

---

#### Historical Overview

The area reached its boom in the 1920s, becoming a midwestern center of food commerce attracting over a million customers a year. The most important actor in the formation of the district was the Fulton Street Wholesale Market Company, constructing buildings to house meat wholesalers — a mix of small local businesses and larger companies all specializing in wholesale produce and meatpacking.

The Great Depression hit the market hard in the 1930s. Technological development meant that refrigeration no longer had to be centralized in an urban setting, and the city could not sustain large truck movement within urban streets. Through the mid-20th century the area declined. The demographic transformation in the decade from 2010 to 2018 indicates the current restructuring: the population grew significantly, the racial makeup of residents shifted, and white and Asian population growth aligned with the area's corporate transformation.

#### Walking Tour

![Map of Walking Tour stops along W. Fulton Market Street]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_02_fmid_map.png)
{:.image}
Map of Walking Tour stops along W. Fulton Market Street. The tour begins at the Gateway and proceeds west along the corridor. (Google Maps, 2025)
{:.caption}

The walking tour takes members through Fulton Market street, starting from the Fulton Market Gateway and proceeding west. Each stop identifies and acknowledges buildings based on what they currently are as well as what they used to be. This introduces both the current and historical identity of the neighborhood, including stops that feature histories that have been completely demolished as well as ones that have been successfully repurposed.

#### Stop 1: Fulton Market Gateway — W. Fulton Market St. at N. Halsted St.

![Fulton Market Gateway sign and streetscape]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_04_gateway.png)
{:.image}
The Fulton Market Gateway sign on W. Fulton Market Street — a colorful marker of the district's branded reinvention. (Google Earth, 2025)
{:.caption}

The Fulton Market Gateway is the perfect landmark to begin the tour. Its vibrant, colorful nature symbolizes the life rejuvenated into the neighborhood through the current array of establishments. On the other hand, it acts as a prime example of how the area is being turned into a "theme park" — urban redevelopment that prioritizes high-end consumers, residents, and tourists. It marks the luxurious environment that now defines the community, and signals the broader transformation of Fulton Market from an industrial working district into a curated, high-end destination.

![Fulton Market Innovation District Plan map]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_05_fmid_plan.png)
{:.image}
The Fulton Market Innovation District (FMID) plan — one of the most important city-funded actions to formalize investment and redevelopment in the area. (Commission on Chicago Landmarks, 2015)
{:.caption}

The gateway sign was installed as part of the Fulton Market Innovation District Plan, one of the most important actions funded and regulated by the city of Chicago to formalize investment and redevelopment in the area. The plan aimed to "preserve existing jobs while accommodating private sector investments." As of the early 2010s, the area was designated for high-end development, prioritizing profit-making private sector investment. The policy essentially sets the area for exclusively upscale development, demonstrating how the city prioritizes economic growth over existing community preservation.

This is also in line with the area being designated as a Chicago City Landmark. This designation aimed at protecting the historical significance of the area, evaluating proposed building efforts through a lens of protecting "significant historical and architectural features."

#### Stop 2: 800 W. Fulton Street — Former Isaacson & Stein Fish Co.

![Isaacson & Stein Fish Co. building before demolition]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_06_isaacson_stein.png)
{:.image}
Isaacson & Stein Fish Co. building on W. Fulton Street — Chicago's oldest fish wholesaler, demolished in 2019 to make way for a 19-story office tower. (Stephanie Lulay / DNAinfo, 2019)
{:.caption}

Currently, 800 W. Fulton is home to a brand new sky-high office building. The former two-story structure built and used for food sale was demolished. Despite the demolition, this site is still significant because of what it used to house. Isaacson & Stein Fish Co. was Chicago's oldest fish wholesaler, operating for over 80 years as a fish market. After the market stopped operating, the area was first repurposed as a nightclub in the 1980s and recently torn down to become an office tower with $95 million in investments.

This site is a prime example of how restructuring in the area is inevitably driven by economic considerations. It also illustrates how demolitions result in a loss of communal memory and shared history. A frequent visitor noted: "Watching this all come down is devastating because there is nothing like it and there never will [be again]. Because quite frankly those businesses have been pushed out" (Pena, 2019).

One interesting preservation question: can we automatically regard this as a "failure" just because it was demolished? The building was built for food maintenance and sale and used to this end for years. However, if such food sales never take place in the area, the building will wear down. The question is whether it is inherently wrong to tear down a building that has accomplished its purpose — or whether maintaining it would be less an act of preservation and more an obstacle to the neighborhood's evolving needs.

#### Stop 3: Beatrix Restaurant — W. Fulton Market St. (former Latino Meat & Produce Inc.)

![Latino Meat & Produce Inc. before conversion to Beatrix Restaurant]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_07_latino_meat.png)
{:.image}
The building at this location as Latino Meat & Produce Inc. — a small minority-owned business displaced by the upscale Beatrix Restaurant. (Google Earth, 2025)
{:.caption}

Beatrix Restaurant is a prime example of the upscale culinary scene established in Fulton Market in the last decade, and also an example of how buildings in the area were renovated and modernized while still maintaining the overall architectural integrity of the original building. The building material was kept as red brick, in line with the style of the entire neighborhood. The storefronts maintained the square, store-like style. Even though the building is now used as a restaurant, the front-facing design still hints at a market. New floors were built, but the spaced-out rectangular window form was maintained.

Despite the building's success in respecting industrial history through architectural form, the transformation has significant social implications. The building historically belonged to "Latino Meat & Produce Inc." — a small business owned by minority, low-income individuals. The replacement of this business with a high-end, luxurious restaurant shows how these communities were displaced. This stop is a clear example of gentrification: a long-standing, working-class community driven out to make space for incoming, wealthy businesses — resulting not only in the loss of these businesses but also a loss of diversity in the area.

In this sense, the building provides an important preservation question: a clash between architectural preservation and the preservation of communities. Inflow of new wealthy business owners ensures the longevity of the built environment at the stake of cultural erasure.

#### Stop 4: Fulton Market Row — W. Fulton Market St. between Peoria and Green Streets

![Fulton Market Row current and historic]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_08_fulton_row.png)
{:.image}
Fulton Market Row: current streetscape (left) and historic photograph, 1959 (right). The buildings between Peoria and Green Streets were built by the Fulton Street Wholesale Market Company in the early 1900s. (Google Earth, 2025 / John McCarthy, 1959)
{:.caption}

This series of buildings is where the Fulton Street Wholesale Market Company started its operations in the early 1900s. These buildings are very typical of the architecture and representative of the overall built environment of the neighborhood. The Sanborn map of West Fulton Market depicts building materials and composition as well as the economic activity taking place. Buildings are made from tile with wood posts, typically two or three stories with basements. The building was home to 22 vendors selling goods varying from fish to poultry.

Buildings are narrow, uniform, and have relatively similar fronts. The tile and brick construction added to sanitation and fire resistance, keeping a cool indoors — essential for food storage. Besides architectural significance, the market has cultural value as one of the earliest and most successfully operating marketplace establishments in the Midwest. Standard Guide to Chicago for 1891 describes the market as "one of the most interesting sights in the city," admiring it for "its cleanliness, the neatness and the perfect business management of the great exchange."

#### Stop 5: Time Out Market Chicago — 916 W. Fulton Market

![Time Out Market Chicago exterior]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_09_timeout.png)
{:.image}
Time Out Market Chicago at 916 W. Fulton Market — a prime example of adaptive reuse that honors the neighborhood's food market heritage. (Tripadvisor, 2025)
{:.caption}

The Time Out Market is a one-of-a-kind example of modernizing and transforming the wholesale market culture to current design and commerce standards. The building indoors is set up as a market, housing 18 vendors in a large open food hall. The design honors historical ties to the neighborhood: the deliberate use of "blackened steel and poured concrete floors" is a direct reflection of the utilitarian origins of both the building and the neighborhood. This is harmonized with more modern installations such as vertically-installed tiles on bar fronts.

A prime example of adaptive reuse, the Time Out Market in both its design, setup, and product offerings manages to embody the culinary-forward past of Fulton Market while emphasizing the culture authentic to Chicago. This is evident through a large "Chicago" tile installation and black-and-white pictures from the food hall dating to the 19th century. History-forward communication is also present through the vendors: visitors can purchase items reflecting authentic Chicago history such as "Original Chicago" hot dogs and beef sandwiches.

The structure currently used as the Time Out Market was previously occupied by El Cubano's Wholesale Meats — a prime example of the working-class communities that occupied this area in its origin. An interview with Rolando Casimiro details how he remembers "there were twice as many semis lined up on the street, trucks ready to deliver and ship barnyards of pork and poultry." He and many similar descendants of families owning businesses in the area point out how "What's left of the meatpacking business here is nothing," and "what remains is a neighborhood changing for the better — and totally bougie" (Borelli, 2019).

From a preservation perspective, buildings need an ongoing and sustainable purpose that fits in with the ever-changing economic and social demands of the environment. The replacement of El Cubano's by the Time Out Market can be interpreted as a necessary transformation for the sake of preserving the built environment, while acknowledging that economic factors are among the biggest drivers of modern-day preservation.

#### Stop 6: Nealey Foods — 900-918 W. Fulton Street

![Nealey Foods building at 900-918 W. Fulton Street]({{site.baseurl}}/images/koyuncul_10_nealey_foods.png)
{:.image}
Nealey Foods at 900-918 W. Fulton Street — a mid-20th century food wholesaler awaiting demolition and redevelopment. (Google Earth, 2025)
{:.caption}

Nealey Foods is a very large food wholesaler that has been in operation since the mid-20th century. Walter Nealey started it as a small poultry business; his son took over in the 1980s to incorporate and grow the corporation. The building portrays years of history tying back to the industrial and culinary roots of the neighborhood.

This building is a significant stop because Nealey Foods is the first structure in the tour that is awaiting demolition without having been renovated or reused. The Commission on Chicago Landmarks has recently approved the demolition of the building. The aim is for it to be "rehabilitated and renovated, featuring a new retail storefront" and replaced by a "five story mixed-use structure" (Preservation Chicago, 2024).

The purchase and planned renovation illustrate each step of the transformation of the built environment. Nealey Foods, once a successful family-owned business, no longer operates. The property is purchased for high prices by high-end developers. Preservation Chicago describes the outcome as a "win" since the front façade will be preserved and the building will be partially restored, retaining some historic fabric — raising the question of what partial facade preservation achieves when the use and community are entirely replaced.

#### Stop 7: Google Headquarters / 1K Fulton — 1000 W. Fulton Market

The Google Headquarters building, also known as 1K Fulton, is a significant stop reflecting the transformation of the area today and the future direction toward which the Fulton Market is headed. Google's move to the Fulton Market district was one of the first examples of rezoning the district to make space for incoming profitable corporate residents. The building converts a 1920s industrial structure, originally built as cold storage for meat maintenance, into a super-modern office building. The project is described as "one of the most ambitious architectural examples of 'adaptive re-use' to date" (Santisi, 2014).

The design features of the building don't exactly reflect the neighborhood's history or its meatpacking origins, contrary to some other development efforts in the area. One of the biggest consequences of Google's move has been skyrocketing property prices. Google's move initiated an influx of numerous other corporate firms, bringing a high-income workforce and resulting in increased housing prices. The Google HQ is therefore a prime symbol of gentrification in the area, ensuring access to housing is close to impossible for communities previously residing in the neighborhood.

---

#### Conclusion

This walking tour of Fulton Market portrays a clear lesson in preservation studies: there is a tradeoff between preserving the built environment and maintaining communities, cultural memory, and social layers. The urban landscape of cities keeps changing — residents, trends, businesses, social life, and industries now transform faster than ever. We must therefore learn the best way to tackle this unavoidable transformation rather than resist it, looking for ways to best practice preservation.

Among the stops, the Time Out Market aligns most closely with a successful approach to preservation. The renovation reflected historical aesthetics while modernizing and adapting to current demand. The newly found purpose carries significant reference and ties to both the building and neighborhood history — still used as a food "market" of some sort, but reimagined for modern demand. The insertion of historical pictures furthers thematic continuity between past and present.

By contrast, the Isaacson & Stein Building, demolished and replaced by an office building with no historical ties, is a preservation failure — not because of the demolition itself, but because the new establishment features no references to what used to be there. This walking tour clearly teaches preservationists that modernization is inevitable and purpose drives livelihood for buildings. However, it is possible to balance reflection of historical memory with renovation activities.
