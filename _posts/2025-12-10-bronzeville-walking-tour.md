#### Introduction

Bronzeville sits within the Grand Boulevard community area on Chicago's South Side. Originally settled by European immigrants — some of whom grew wealthy through railroads and meatpacking and built elaborately decorated mansions — the neighborhood transformed during the Great Migration as Black residents from the South arrived in search of work and freedom. Chicago's central position in the national railroad network made it a natural destination. By 1916, Bronzeville was predominantly Black.

The neighborhood soon became what residents called the "Black Metropolis": a concentration of institutions offering services unavailable or denied to Black communities elsewhere — Binga Bank (1905), the *Chicago Bee*, the nationally circulated *Chicago Defender*, and a vibrant entertainment district of nightclubs and dance halls that made Bronzeville a mandatory stop for performers and political figures from the 1920s through the 1950s.

Decline came from two directions. The Dan Ryan Expressway (1962), built under Mayor Richard Daley, physically severed Bronzeville from surrounding neighborhoods. The end of racially restricted housing then pushed middle-class residents out, while low-income residential towers replaced the neighborhood's finer mixed-use fabric. Congress designated the Bronzeville–Black Metropolis a National Heritage Area in 2023, providing up to $10 million in federal funding over fifteen years for conservation, management, and development.

The six stops on this tour form a rectangle around 43rd and 47th Streets, exploring how preservation, adaptive reuse, and loss have shaped the neighborhood's current landscape.

#### Tour Stops

**1. The Forum**
*318 E 43rd St*

![The Forum, Bronzeville]({{site.baseurl}}/images/davalos_01_the_forum.png)

Built in 1897 and designed by Samuel Atwater Treat, the Forum began as a community hall for political speeches and rallies. As Bronzeville grew into the Black Metropolis, it became a venue for civil rights organizing and a premier stage for Black performers — Nat King Cole and Muddy Waters among them. It sits one block from the 43rd Street CTA station, which opened in 1892 as part of the South Side Elevated Railroad built to serve the World's Columbian Exposition. The Forum fell vacant in the 1990s and faced demolition until community protest reached the alderman. In 2011, South Side entrepreneur Bernard Loyd purchased it through his nonprofit Urban Juncture and has been working to restore it and return community events to the space.

---

**2. Louis Armstrong House**
*421 E 44th St*

Louis Armstrong lived in this Bronzeville home during his Chicago years after leaving New Orleans, becoming part of the community of artists who defined the Black Metropolis's cultural moment. The home is a private residence and not open to visitors; a plaque marks its significance, noting Armstrong's Chicago techniques and career. That it remains a lived-in building rather than an institutional space is part of what preserves it: active use sustains the structure without the funding pressures that weigh on larger venues.

---

**3. Parkway Ballroom**
*4455 S Martin Luther King Dr*

The building's ground floor originally housed the Chicago Metropolitan Mutual Assurance Company (CMMAC), which opened in 1940 to offer insurance to Black clients at a time when most insurers refused or degraded coverage for them. The Parkway Ballroom occupied the second floor and appeared in the *Green Book*, the guide for Black travelers navigating segregation-era America. A 1949 expansion added a restaurant and office space. CMMAC sold in 1991 as racial integration eroded its specialized market; the ballroom closed in 1974 and the building sat vacant for eleven years. Purchased in 2002, it was reopened as an event space and now also houses the Bronzeville Historical Society. The recent addition of ParkwaySocial, a coworking lounge within the historic building, continues its evolution through adaptive reuse — the same structure serving successive community needs without being frozen in time.

---

**4. Palm Tavern Site (Demolished)**
*420 E 47th St*

The Palm Tavern is included as a counter-example — one of Bronzeville's significant losses. A sister to Parkway Ballroom, the Tavern served celebrities and middle- and upper-class residents during Bronzeville's peak. Manager Gerri Oliver had run it since 1956. In 1998, it was sold to a new owner and seized by the city in 2001 for building code violations; Oliver had been living on the premises. A planned initiative to transform 47th Street into "Tobacco Road" — a cultural destination anchored by restored venues — never materialized. The Palm was demolished in 2004. The lot now holds a sculpture garden honoring the Great Migration; significant as public space, but disconnected from the specific history the Tavern embodied. Its loss illustrates how quickly the window for preservation can close once a building's situation begins to spiral.

---

**5. Rosenwald Courts Apartments**
*4600 S Michigan Ave*

Built in 1929 as the Michigan Boulevard Garden Apartments by philanthropist Julius Rosenwald to provide quality housing for Black families arriving during the Great Migration. The complex fell into disrepair and was vacated in 2000, but community groups and preservationists campaigned to save it; a full rehabilitation between 2015 and 2016 restored the facades, revitalized the courtyard, and added retail and office space. It now operates as mixed-income affordable and senior housing. The Rosenwald demonstrates that historic preservation can directly serve contemporary housing needs — that rehabilitating existing housing stock rather than replacing it allows communities to retain both population and place-based identity. Its renovation kept the essential elements: the architecture and its type of resident.

---

**6. Chicago Urban League and Swift Mansion**
*4510 S Michigan Ave*

The Swift Mansion (1892, Richardsonian Romanesque) was built for a wealthy meatpacking family and became the Chicago Urban League's headquarters in the 1960s. When the League outgrew the 10,000-square-foot mansion in the 1980s, board members proposed moving downtown. League president James Compton refused: "We would lose our identity in the community if we were on X-numbered floor in an office building downtown. We would be abandoning the community we serve." In 1984, a modern steel-and-glass building designed by a Black architect with ties to IIT — also in Bronzeville — was built adjacent to the Mansion to serve as the new headquarters. The two buildings stand together: a record of institutional growth that stayed rooted in the community it serves. Swift Mansion itself was used by other nonprofits until a 2023 fire; it appeared on Preservation Chicago's Most Endangered list for 2024 and currently faces the same uncertain future the Forum faced a decade ago.

#### Archival and Map Analysis

Sanborn fire insurance maps of the 43rd Street corridor (Insurance Maps of Chicago, Vol. 14) document the Forum as a two-story brick hall with stage, scenery space, and a firewall facing the back alley. The surrounding blocks show dense residential fabric — predominantly single-family and multifamily dwellings — interspersed with storefronts including drugstores, a cobbler, and a dry cleaner. The 43rd Street station appears alongside bakeries and additional commercial frontage, marking the street as a mixed-use commercial spine.

Community settlement maps from 1900 and 1950 (City of Chicago Department of Development and Planning) show the Great Migration in spatial terms: the 1900 map documents Black settlement already concentrated in the Bronzeville area alongside Austrian, Swedish, and German immigrant populations; by 1950, the area is solidly Black while European immigrant populations have dispersed to the suburban periphery. A 1934 economic census map shows the income diversity of the period — a mix of low, middle, and high economic classifications within the same neighborhood, consistent with oral histories describing the neighborhood's class range, from its elaborate mansions to its modest apartment blocks.

#### Interview: Bernard and Kaden Loyd, Urban Juncture / Build Bronzeville

As part of this project, the author attended the Forum during Chicago Open House and subsequently interviewed Bernard Loyd (who purchased the Forum in 2011) and Kaden Loyd, his son, who grew up in Bronzeville and is involved in current revitalization efforts.

Bernard Loyd on physical spaces and historical memory: "If the Forum had not been there... you would not have had that experience. They would not have introduced you to certain elements of the community, to information about the community, information that is triggered by a physical place that is embodied because that physical place housed events, because that physical place catalyzed activity, because that physical place was a destination."

On capital as the central constraint: "A key challenge is the resources to actually do so... In communities that have been disinvested in for decades, this becomes a fundamental challenge to maintaining buildings. Capital, therefore, is a central challenge in making preservation possible."

Kaden Loyd on new development across from the Forum (43rd Green): "One of the biggest frustrations for me with 43 Green was it's the ugliest building ever built. In one of the most gorgeous communities... Who's to say you can't still maximize profit while keeping a similar design, architecture, structure, building materials, or interior?"

---

#### Conclusion

Bronzeville's infrastructure — large residential housing stock, dense commercial corridors, performance venues, and civic institutions — was ideally suited to the community that formed within it during the Great Migration. The neighborhood's designation as a National Heritage Area in 2023 provides new tools. The tour's six stops show a range of outcomes: adaptive reuse that sustained relevance (Parkway Ballroom), rehabilitation that served both preservation and housing equity (Rosenwald Courts), institutional continuity through expansion rather than relocation (Urban League), and loss that foreclosed possibility (Palm Tavern). The Forum remains in the balance, caught between the capital constraints that have stalled its restoration and the cultural memory that motivates the effort to save it. Walking through Bronzeville block by block — rather than pointing to any single landmark — is what makes its layered history legible.
