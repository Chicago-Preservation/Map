#### Introduction

Woodlawn's profound history is largely absent from the National Register of Historic Places, with only two buildings officially designated within its boundaries. This tour treats that absence not as a deficit but as an argument: Woodlawn's significance is encoded in everyday stories, community spaces, and acts of resistance, not in preserved monuments. Two guiding objectives shape the route — giving voice to those historically excluded from preservation narratives, and tracing how Woodlawn's past connects to its present and future.

#### Historical Context

Woodlawn began as Dutch immigrant farmland in the mid-nineteenth century, annexed by Chicago in 1889 with a population under 1,000. The 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in neighboring Jackson Park triggered a development and population boom. After the Exposition, Woodlawn entered economic decline, revitalized briefly by the construction of 63rd Street as a commercial and transit corridor.

Middle-class Black families migrated to Woodlawn from the overcrowded Black Belt, but opportunistic landlords subdivided apartments into cramped "kitchenette" units and charged inflated rents. By mid-century, approximately 89% of Woodlawn's population was Black. The assassination of Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. in 1968 accelerated the departure of white business owners; fires destroyed 362 abandoned buildings between 1968 and 1971. Population loss, unemployment, and disinvestment followed.

#### Archival Work

Sanborn fire insurance maps from 1926 and 1975 document the neighborhood's structural transformation. The 1926 edition shows dense two- and three-bedroom family housing surrounded by local shops and churches. The 1975 edition replaces much of that housing with vacant lots and commercial uses — a direct material record of the arson and displacement of the intervening decades.

Photographic archives trace 63rd Street's parallel transformation: from the thriving commercial strip documented in a 1951 *Chicago History Museum* photograph, to the quieter corridor visible in a 1959 traffic board image, to the near-empty streetscape photographed in 1987 by the Library of Congress.

#### Tour Stops

**1. Lorraine Hansberry House**
*6140 S Rhodes Ave*

![Lorraine Hansberry House]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_01_hansberry_house.png)

Carl Hansberry, a real estate developer, purchased this building in 1937 — against threats of violence — to challenge the racially restrictive covenant that barred Black families from the neighborhood. The Hansberry family lived here from May 26, 1937, to 1940, pursuing a legal challenge that ended in a favorable Supreme Court ruling (*Hansberry v. Lee*). Lorraine Hansberry later drew on her experience in this house for *A Raisin in the Sun*, the first Broadway production by a Black playwright. In a 1964 letter to the *New York Times*, she described bricks thrown through windows, her mother keeping a gun, and being beaten on the way to school. The house was designated a Chicago Landmark on February 10, 2010.

---

**2. Emmett & Mamie Till-Mobley House**
*6427 S St Lawrence Ave*

![Emmett & Mamie Till-Mobley House]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_02_till_house.png)

The Till family moved here from Mississippi in November 1951, settling on the second floor of this two-story flat. Emmett Till spent summers helping on his uncle's farm in Mississippi; on August 28, 1955, he was abducted and murdered for allegedly whistling at a white woman. His mother, Mamie Till, contacted the *Chicago Defender* and chose to display her son's beaten body publicly, circulating photographs that became catalysts for the civil rights movement. The home is now a museum commemorating both Emmett Till and Mamie Till-Mobley's decades of activism.

---

**3. Daley's Restaurant**
*809 E 63rd St*

![Daley's Restaurant]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_03_daleys_restaurant.png)

Founded in 1892 by Irish immigrant ironworker John Daley to feed construction workers during the Columbian Exposition buildup, Daley's is Chicago's oldest continuously operating eatery. Sold in 1918 to Greek immigrants Paul Emmanuel and Tom Kyros, who preserved the name and spirit, the restaurant survived the 1932 banking collapse, reopened in 1937, and endured through multiple decades of neighborhood upheaval. A new location on S Cottage Grove Ave opened in 2018.

---

**4. 63rd Street**
*Corridor along E 63rd St*

63rd Street is not a formal stop but warrants extended discussion en route. After the 1893 World's Fair, it became a bustling commercial strip with department stores, theaters, and transit connections. As middle-class Black families arrived, it transformed into a vibrant Black economic and cultural district — restaurants, beauty shops, bookstores, jazz venues. Mid-century racial transition brought business closures and predatory practices; the destruction of the 'L' tracks in the 1990s further isolated the corridor. Current community initiatives seek to restore 63rd Street as a commercial anchor, viewing its revival as essential to neighborhood-wide economic recovery.

---

**5. Robust Coffee Lounge**
*6300 S Cottage Grove Ave*

![Robust Coffee Lounge]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_05_robust_coffee.png)

An adaptive reuse story: the brick building was originally industrial, converted to mixed-use lofts in 2008. When the housing market collapsed, owner Jack Sapstein repurposed the ground floor as a coffee shop. Robust opened in 2010 and has since served as one of the neighborhood's primary gathering spaces, linking Hyde Park and Woodlawn residents. Upper-floor housing units remain but are not currently rented.

---

**6. St. Gelasius Church (formerly Shrine of Christ)**
*6206 S Woodlawn Ave*

![St. Gelasius Church]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_06_st_gelasius.png)

The parish of St. Clara was founded in 1894 for a German immigrant congregation. Designed by German architect Henry J. Schlacks to "reflect the majesty and glory of Rome," the church thrived through the 1920s Irish Catholic community. As Woodlawn became majority Black after *Hansberry v. Lee*, pastor Father Tracy O'Sullivan engaged in gang outreach and advocated for nondiscriminatory housing. Fires in 1976 and 2015 destroyed much of the interior; the second fire led to closure and a planned demolition that was halted by community protest. The church received landmark status and is currently undergoing interior and roof renovation.

---

**7. First Presbyterian Church of Chicago**
*6400 S Kimbark Ave*

![First Presbyterian Church of Chicago]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_07_first_presbyterian.png)

Organized in June 1833 — two months before Chicago's incorporation — First Church is the city's oldest continuously operating institution. The current American Gothic cathedral at 64th and Kimbark dates from 1926. In 1965, the church engaged with the Black P Stone Nation gang as part of SCLC-supported outreach. After the 1968–71 arson wave, First Church purchased abandoned lots and converted them to community gardens, including the 65th and Woodlawn Garden, and in 1981 built a solar greenhouse in partnership with the Center for Neighborhood Technology.

---

**8. 61st Street Farmers Market**
*6100 S Blackstone Ave*

![61st Street Farmers Market]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_08_farmers_market.png)

Hosted by Experimental Station since 2008, the outdoor market is positioned deliberately on the Hyde Park–Woodlawn border as a community bridge. Produce is sourced from South Side urban agriculture projects. During winter, operations move indoors to Experimental Station's multi-use space, which also hosts community dinners and a youth bicycle program. The market exemplifies the tour's central argument: ephemeral, community-serving structures can hold as much architectural significance as formally designated monuments.

---

**9. Hyde Park Academy High School**
*6220 S Stony Island Ave*

![Hyde Park Academy High School]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_09_hyde_park_academy.png)

Established 1863; the current classical-style building opened 1914 with subsequent modernizations in 1938, 1965, and 1972. Designated a magnet school and Career Academy in 1976, and officially renamed Hyde Park Academy in 1996. Became an authorized IB World School in 2001. Notable alumni include Amelia Earhart, Gwendolyn Brooks (first Black winner of the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry), and Minnie Riperton.

---

**10. Statue of the Republic (The Golden Lady)**
*Jackson Park, Hayes Dr*

![Statue of the Republic]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_10_statue_republic.png)

The 24-foot gilded bronze replica of Daniel Chester French's 65-foot original — one of the most iconic features of the 1893 Columbian Exposition — was commissioned in 1918 by the B.F. Ferguson Fund and Women's World Fair Fund to mark the Exposition's twenty-fifth anniversary. The original was destroyed by fire shortly after the fair. The replica was re-gilded and rededicated in 1993 (centennial) and granted landmark status in 2003. It closes the tour as both geographic anchor and symbol: a smaller, resilient copy dedicated to memory, standing at the edge of the park where Woodlawn's first major boom began.

---

#### Conclusion

Woodlawn's history is marked by the initial boom of the Columbian Exposition, the discriminatory housing practices that produced the "kitchenettes," the devastating 1968–71 arson, and the persistent self-determination that followed. The neighborhood's virtual absence from the National Register reflects the canonical biases of traditional preservation practice, which has favored grand permanent architecture over community-scaled and ephemeral spaces. Revitalization efforts — the renewed focus on 63rd Street, the preservation of St. Gelasius Church — are real but must be understood in relation to prior displacement patterns. Woodlawn's future depends on revitalization that is in conversation with the neighborhood's long-term residents, rooted in equitable housing, and built on the foundation of its untold history.
