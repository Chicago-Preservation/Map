Woodlawn's significance is often overshadowed by the enormous institutional presence of the adjacent University of Chicago, and by the scarcity of tangible, monumental examples of its past. Unlike neighborhoods where history is etched into preserved architecture, Woodlawn's enduring weight lies instead in the everyday stories, struggles, and triumphs of its inhabitants. Only two buildings in Woodlawn are listed on the National Register of Historic Places — a strikingly low count that, rather than limiting this tour, transformed it: Woodlawn presented itself not as a fully charted historical space, but as a blank slate whose rich, complex story could be re-established and retold.

Woodlawn was originally Dutch immigrant farmland, annexed by Chicago in 1889. The 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in neighboring Jackson Park spurred a boom in development and population. After the exposition, economic decline set in until 63rd Street was built to attract middle-class Black families migrating out of the Black Belt. Opportunistic landlords illegally subdivided apartments into cramped "kitchenettes," driving up rents while crowding Black residents. By mid-century the neighborhood was 89% Black. After Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s assassination, racial tensions boiled over; fires destroyed hundreds of buildings between 1968 and 1971, sending the population into sharp decline. This tour gives voice to those who have historically been deprived of one, and tells a story that links Woodlawn's diverse past to its present and future.

## Stop 1: Lorraine Hansberry House
**6140 S. Rhodes Avenue**

![The Lorraine Hansberry House, a brick two-flat with front porch on South Rhodes Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_01_hansberry_house.png)

This is where Lorraine Hansberry's family lived from May 26, 1937, to 1940, while fighting a legal battle that ultimately reached the Supreme Court. Hansberry's father, Carl, purchased the building in 1937 to challenge the racially discriminatory housing covenant that barred his family from moving into this then-white neighborhood. Lorraine depicted the experience in a 1964 letter to the *New York Times*, describing bricks thrown through windows, her mother keeping a gun for protection, and being beaten on the way to school. She drew on this home when writing *A Raisin in the Sun*, the first Broadway production by an African American playwright. The house was designated a Chicago Landmark in 2010.

## Stop 2: Emmett & Mamie Till-Mobley House
**6427 S. St. Lawrence Avenue**

![The Till-Mobley House, a two-story brick building on South St. Lawrence Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_02_till_house.png)

The Till family migrated from Mississippi in November 1951 and moved into the second floor of this two-story flat. Emmett Till routinely spent his summers back in Mississippi to help on his uncle's farm. On August 28, 1955, at around 2:30 a.m., Bryant and Milam arrived armed at his uncle's home and abducted Emmett for allegedly whistling at Bryant's wife. His body was discovered in the Tallahatchie River three days later. When Mamie Till learned what happened, she immediately contacted the *Chicago Defender* and shared photographs of her son's beaten body, circulating them nationally. The murder sparked the Civil Rights movement. The family home has since been turned into a museum commemorating Emmett Till and his mother Mamie, who became a civil rights activist after her son's death.

## Stop 3: Daley's Restaurant
**809 E. 63rd Street**

![Daley's Restaurant exterior on East 63rd Street, signage reading "Since 1892"]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_03_daleys_restaurant.png)

Daley's is Chicago's oldest eatery, founded in 1892 by immigrant ironworker John Daley to feed construction workers during the building of the University of Chicago and preparation for the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition. In 1918, Greek immigrants Paul Emmanuel and Tom Kyros purchased the restaurant, preserving its name and character. The bank financing their 1932 remodel failed; by 1937 they had saved enough to reopen, renaming it New Daley's Restaurant. The restaurant's success carried it through multiple decades, economic periods, and political movements. In 2018, construction began on a new location nearby on South Cottage Grove Avenue. Daley's is a physical reminder of the resilience of Woodlawn.

## Stop 4: Robust Coffee Lounge
**6352 S. Ingleside Avenue**

![Robust Coffee Lounge, a brick mixed-use loft building on South Ingleside Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_05_robust_coffee.png)

Robust was originally an industrial building renovated and adapted into a mixed-use loft in 2008. When the housing market crashed, the building's owner Jack Sapstein converted the first floor into a coffee shop. Robust officially opened in 2010 and has been influential in linking Hyde Park and Woodlawn residents. It serves as a community anchor and a reminder of the importance of adaptive reuse: an ordinary industrial building transformed into a thriving third space that bridges two neighborhoods.

## Stop 5: Shrine of Christ / St. Gelasius Church
**6400 S. Kimbark Avenue**

![St. Gelasius Church, a historic Catholic church with bell tower on South Kimbark Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_06_st_gelasius.png)

The parish of St. Clara was founded in 1894 to serve a largely German community that immigrated to Woodlawn amid the boom of the World's Columbian Exposition. The church was designed by German architect Henry J. Schlacks to reflect the majesty of Rome. By the 1920s the neighborhood was largely Irish, and the parish thrived. As Woodlawn became majority African American after the *Hansberry v. Lee* ruling prohibited racially restrictive real estate covenants, Father Tracy O'Sullivan engaged in gang outreach and worked for nondiscriminatory affordable housing. A 1976 fire destroyed most of the original interior. In 1990, after a merger, the parish was renamed St. Gelasius — for a pope of African heritage — in recognition of its congregation. A second fire in 2015 led to closure and a demolition threat, but community protests saved it and it gained landmark status. Preservation is ongoing.

## Stop 6: First Presbyterian Church of Chicago
**64th Street & Kimbark Avenue**

![First Presbyterian Church of Chicago, an American Gothic cathedral at 64th and Kimbark Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_07_first_presbyterian.png)

Organized in June 1833 — two months before Chicago was incorporated — First Presbyterian is the oldest continuously operating institution in the city. It was also the site of the city's first public school, and its founder Eliza Emily Chappel educated both settlers and Native Americans, aided the wounded in the Civil War, and participated in the Underground Railroad. In 1926, First merged with Woodlawn Park Presbyterian and built a new American Gothic cathedral at 64th and Kimbark. The church later engaged with the Black P Stone Nation gang through the SCLC and purchased abandoned lots burned out in the 1968–1971 fires, turning three into community gardens. In 1978 it began work on a solar greenhouse completed in 1981. The church still serves as a pillar of the community today.

## Stop 7: 61st Street Farmers Market
**61st Street & Dorchester Avenue**

![61st Street Farmers Market, an outdoor market at the corner of 61st and Dorchester with vendor tents and shoppers]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_08_farmers_market.png)

The outdoor 61st Street Farmers Market is hosted by Experimental Station and launched in 2008. Intentionally located on the border of Hyde Park and Woodlawn, it serves as a bridge between the two neighborhoods. Produce is primarily harvested from South Side urban agriculture projects and sold to give community members access to fresh food. During winter the market moves inside, and Experimental Station hosts community dinners, youth bicycle programs, and public events. This stop is intended to reframe how we view a neighborhood's structural identity: ephemeral structures that serve a community can be more critical to its architectural fabric than permanent landmarks.

## Stop 8: Hyde Park Academy High School
**6220 S. Stony Island Avenue**

![Hyde Park Academy High School, a classical-style stone building with wide steps on South Stony Island Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_09_hyde_park_academy.png)

Established in 1863, Hyde Park High School began as a single room in a local grammar school. After several relocations, it moved into its current permanent structure in 1914, undergoing major reconstruction in 1938, 1965, and 1972. In 1976 it became both a magnet school and Career Academy; since 1996 it has been known as Hyde Park Academy, centering comprehensive college readiness and career preparation. Notable alumni include Amelia Earhart, Gwendolyn Brooks (first African American to win a Pulitzer Prize for poetry), and Minnie Riperton. The school achieved International Baccalaureate World School status in 2001.

## Stop 9: Statue of the Republic Replica
**Jackson Park**

![The Statue of the Republic replica, a 24-foot gilded bronze sculpture in Jackson Park]({{site.baseurl}}/images/smith_10_statue_republic.png)

The Golden Lady was built in 1918, commissioned by the B.F. Ferguson Fund and the Women's World Fair Fund to commemorate the 25th anniversary of the World's Columbian Exposition. It is a 24-foot gilded bronze replica — much smaller than Daniel Chester French's original 65-foot statue, which burned shortly after the fair. The original was among the nation's tallest sculptures at the time of the 1893 Exposition, second only to the Statue of Liberty. The replica was re-gilded and rededicated in 1993 and granted landmark status in 2003. It closes this tour by bringing Woodlawn's history full circle: a remnant of the economic boom that first put the neighborhood on the map, motivating its pursuit of that prosperity once again.

---

*Woodlawn's history is not a narrative of static preservation, but one of cyclical upheaval and persistent self-determination. The neighborhood's past — from the Columbian Exposition, to discriminatory housing, to devastating arson — demonstrates how the present, defined by vacant lots alongside vibrant community pillars, emerged from waves of economic and racial struggle. The future of Woodlawn cannot rely on isolated efforts, but on a unified vision where revitalization is rooted in the best interests of its long-term residents.*
