The Berghoff is one of Chicago and surroundings' most notorious German restaurants, due to its authenticity and welcoming environment. Originally started as a beer selling business where each sale would come with a complementary sandwich, The Berghoff expanded into a restaurant, a bar with a brewery and a catering business. Nowadays it even has its own place at O'Hare terminal 1. Flavors have also evolved from traditional German cuisine to appeal to more European palates (History, The Berghoff). Known for its hospitality, family friendly and intergenerational characteristics, The Berghoff claims its status as one of Chicagoland's most beloved legacy businesses."

#### Geography/Neighborhood
The Berghoff is located on the Loop, a neighborhood characterized by the skyscrapers that emerged in the 1880s, post the Great Chicago Fire (1871). The Loop consists of a landscape of brick and metal buildings that define Chicago’s architectural style. Newer buildings are mostly metal based constructions. The Berghoff is found on the block of E Adams St., S Dearborn St., W Jackson Blv. and S State St. Changes to the block have been minimal, with the biggest change being the construction of the US District court at 219 S Dearborn St.

Contrasting physical changes, demographically, the Loop grew throughout the years. A few decades before the establishment of The Berghoff, the Loop’s households estimated around 7,851 (Gerald A. Danzer). Nowadays, the estimation is of 23,894 (CMAP) households. White/caucasian individuals have been the predominant group according to statistics, however the Loop’s diversity has grown immensely, specially with a significant increase of foreign born residents in the year 2000 (Gerald A. Danzer), possibly indicating a migration wave.

In modern days a large variety of languages other than English being spoken in households can be seen, with Spanish and Chinese having leading numbers (CMAP). Some data cannot be compared since the two demographic assessments recorded different types of data. It can be seen that the number of males per 100 women has decreased significantly (Gerald A. Danzer) , possibly indicating a growth in female population as women’s economic power grew, or the inclusion of women in census. Modern data tells us that the Loop is currently mostly inhabited by a population in their early 20s to early 30s, with a household income of 150,000 or more dollars (CMAP).


#### The Family/Community
Herman Joseph Berghoff, born in Dortmund Germany in 1852, made his way to the United States in 1870, when he was only 17 years old. After a series of odd jobs, Herman and his brothers opened a brewery in Fort Wayne, Indiana, in 1882. With the coming of the World Fair to Chicago in 1893, Herman Berghoff saw this as an opportunity to sell his beer at the gates of the fair. The success at the World Fair led Herman to buy a building on 17 West Adams St, where he opened The Berghoff Saloon in 1898 (History, The Berghoff).

Herman Berghoff passed away in 1933, and the ownership of The Berghoff has been passed down through generations of Berghoff family members. Big changes were felt in the restaurant when Carlyn Berghoff took over. With a background in catering, Carlyn originally aimed to change the business into that industry, going as far as changing the name of the restaurant to 17 West Adams Street at the Berghoff. The public feared changes would remove The Berghoff’s iconic environment, which influenced the owners to return to the original name and make minimal changes. These were the creation of a private dining room for catered events and the introduction of more European flavors to the menu, beyond the German cuisine (Walkup, Nation’s Restaurants News).

The Berghoff was the first establishment to receive a liquor license in Chicago after prohibition, and remains to be the first one to receive it every year since then (History, The Berghoff).

The Berghoff remains an iconic establishment, with costumers highlighting its atmosphere and significance in their lives. Customers return after decades being away from Chicago and recount visiting the restaurant with their families, emphasizing the significant role it played in their childhood and relationships with family members. As well as they highlight relationships with the staff, specially servers who have worked for the restaurant for most of their lives (Shelley Zurek, The Berghoff Chicago Review, Now and Then).
