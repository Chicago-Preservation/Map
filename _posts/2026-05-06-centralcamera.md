#### History of the Neighborhood
When Central Camera Co. opened in 1899, the Loop neighborhood was already shifting from residential to commercial use, becoming Chicago’s central business district. For much of the 20th century, the Loop served as a place Chicagoans traveled to for work, shopping, recreation, and civic life. Named for the cable system built to “loop” around the downtown business district in 1897, its development accelerated by the elevated “L” train, with Central Camera Co. located under its tracks. The store contributed to the neighborhood’s commercial environment, becoming a staple for students at the School of the Art Institute and professional photographers working for the city’s newspapers. 
Mid-century urban renewal led to the widespread demolition of historic buildings, including the Cable Building, built the same year Central Camera opened on the same city block, to make way for more modernized and utilitarian architecture. Central Camera’s brick building remained relatively unchanged at its current location until 2020, when it was destroyed during unrest in the Loop following the murder of George Floyd, and later reopened in 2022.

#### The Family/ Community 
After his bar mitzvah at thirteen, Albert Flesch immigrated to America alone from Polár, Hungary, traveling on foot to Venice, Italy, by boat to Ellis Island and by train to Chicago where he had relatives. He found work at Siegel, Cooper, & Co. and, after gaining experience in the photo department, Flesch opened his own camera store, Central Camera Company, in 1899, employing family members as they arrived from Hungary. 
After Albert Flesch’s death in 1933, his sons Harold and Stanley took over the business, expanding the store’s offerings. Harold’s son, Albert “Don” Flesch, began working at the store as a teenager. Following Harold’s death, Don became co-owner with Stanley and is now the sole owner.
Central Camera’s owner continues a legacy of deep involvement in the store and its community, as evidenced in the many newspaper and blog accounts which recount Don Flesch’s warm welcome and how he always remembers both his customers and their camera issues.

#### Business/Commercial
In 1888, George Eastman introduced the Kodak box camera, the first widely available camera for amateur photographers. The market’s expanse allowed Albert Flesch to open Central Camera Co., Chicago’s oldest camera store, where he also offered commercial film development and print processing to his customers. The second generation of the business expanded the type of photographic equipment beyond cameras to products like tripods. Now in its third generation, the store has built a loyal, multigenerational customer base. While the market has changed as smartphone cameras advance, Central Camera remains open through specialty product offerings and its in-store experience, where employees guide customers through buying, selling, and trading new and used digital and film camera equipment. The business has evolved alongside the photographic technology while maintaining its history. In 2020, after the business was destroyed, owner Don Flesch rescued the first camera sold there, the No. 1 Folding Pocket Kodak, from the store’s window. Donations from Central Camera’s built community allowed it to reopen in 2022.
