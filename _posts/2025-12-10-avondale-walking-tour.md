#### Introduction

This walking tour explores how Avondale's built environment — its churches, homes, shops, and parks — embodies over a century of immigration, labor, faith, and demographic changes on Chicago's Northwest Side. The tour is centered along North Milwaukee and West Belmont Avenues. Through it, we trace Avondale's historical arc from an early 1900s manufacturing mecca settled by Polish and other European immigrants, through its mid-century development as a thriving working-class area anchored by Catholic parishes, into late 20th century Latinx migration and industrial decline, and lastly its 21st century state as a mixed-use area still maintaining much of its fascinating historical background.

The tour illustrates several major themes. The most important is immigration and cultural identity — focusing on how churches and ethnic establishments anchored their community through each wave of development. Another key theme is everyday architecture: factories, two-flats, and humble bungalows as symbols of the social fabric of working class communities. Lastly, the tour highlights themes of urban change and resilience through how each generation adapts existing spaces and organizations into modern use.

#### Stop 1: Avondale Park Fieldhouse — 3516 W. School Street

![Tour map of Avondale neighborhood]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_01_map.png)
{:.image}
Tour map showing stops along Milwaukee and Belmont Avenues (Google Maps, 2025)
{:.caption}

The first stop is the Avondale Park Fieldhouse, a neoclassical/colonial revival brick building designed by architect Clarence Hatzfield and built in 1930 under the Irving Park District. The park opened as a fully equipped community space with a playfield, boys' and girls' playgrounds, a wading pool, a sandbox, and tennis courts. Avondale became part of Chicago in 1889 when the city annexed the Town of Jefferson, and throughout the following thirty years, European immigrants — predominantly Polish — settled the area amid a surge of residential development in the 1920s. The land was acquired in 1925 and construction completed by end of 1930.

![Avondale Park Fieldhouse exterior]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_02_avondale_park.png)
{:.image}
Avondale Park Fieldhouse, 3516 W. School Street (Block Club Chicago / Chicago Park District, 2020)
{:.caption}

In 1934, the Great Depression forced twenty-two independent park organizations in Chicago to consolidate into the Chicago Park District. Avondale Park, originally five acres, was significantly reduced when the Kennedy Expressway was built in the 1950s, destroying the northeast portion and eliminating the playfield and tennis courts. The park adapted, adding basketball and volleyball courts and a swimming pool. A major $2.86 million renovation in 2020 modernized the historic fieldhouse, improved ADA accessibility, and enhanced landscaping. Today the park contains an outdoor swimming and spray pool, a playground, and a fieldhouse with four clubrooms, two kitchens, and a gymnastics center — an enduring neighborhood anchor.

#### Stop 2: Frank W. Reilly Elementary School — 3650 W. School Street

Just down the block, the Frank W. Reilly Elementary School was established in the early 20th century and has photographic records dating to 1935. The school was primarily a civic investment for the newly arrived Polish and European immigrant communities working in Avondale's adjacent brickyards and factories. As the area experienced a large influx of Latinx immigrants over the late 20th century, the school's mission shifted accordingly. Today it serves a population that is 93% Latinx, predominantly low-income.

![Two-flat residential housing near Avondale Park and Reilly School]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_03_twoflat_a.png)
{:.image}
Two-flat housing near Avondale Park and Reilly School — characteristic affordable housing of the neighborhood (Google Street View, 2025)
{:.caption}

![Brick residential housing near Reilly School]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_04_twoflat_b.png)
{:.image}
Brick residential context along School Street between Stops 1 and 2 (Google Street View, 2025)
{:.caption}

In 1999, Reilly became the first Chicago Public School to install solar electric panels on its roof. Between these two sites — the park and the school — we see classic two-flats and bungalows acting as naturally affordable housing, their age (pre-1950s) keeping them accessible to successive immigrant communities.

#### Stop 3: Shoemaker Lofts (Former Florsheim Shoe Company Building) — 3963 W. Belmont Avenue

![Shoemaker Lofts exterior and historic Florsheim signage]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_05_shoemaker_lofts.png)
{:.image}
Shoemaker Lofts (left) and preserved Florsheim Shoe Co. signage at the building entrance (Shoemaker Lofts / Azghandi, 2025)
{:.caption}

The former Florsheim Shoe Company Building, today known as Shoemaker Lofts, is among the most architecturally prominent structures in Avondale. The Chicago City Council granted the building official landmark status on March 29th, 2006. Compared to the smaller two-flats and shops surrounding it, the building's physical scale stands as a reminder of the industrial powerhouse that Avondale once was.

The Florsheim Shoe Company began as a Chicago shoe manufacturer in the late 19th century. This building served as a major production site during the company's peak. When the company eventually left, the building sat vacant for years before developers undertook an adaptive reuse conversion into residential condominiums. They paid homage to the building's original use by naming it Shoemaker Lofts, after the renowned shoe company, aiming to preserve the memory of what once stood there — a preservation approach that keeps the industrial past visible through name and building fabric rather than freezing the use itself.

#### Stop 4: St. Joseph Village of Chicago — 4201 W. Belmont Avenue

The St. Joseph Village of Chicago is a retirement home with a deep history rooted in immigration, community support, and relocation. The community was founded by the Franciscan Sisters of Chicago, whose congregation was established in 1894 by Josephine Dudzik — later Sister Mary Theresa — who immigrated from western Poland to Chicago in 1881. After her father's death in 1889, she and her mother began housing abandoned and less fortunate people. By 1898 the congregation had established the St. Joseph Home for the Aged and Crippled, the first and oldest nursing home in the history of Chicago.

The institution expanded over decades, serving not only the elderly but also as a novitiate for the sisters, and hosting sewing and vestment workshops to raise funds. The current St. Joseph Village of Chicago building was established in 2006. Though architecturally modest compared to its predecessor, it preserves the bravery and mission of the Franciscan Sisters. This stop raises a central preservation question: when a building's social purpose survives in a new physical vessel, how do we evaluate what has been preserved and what has been lost?

#### Stop 5: Ballet Folklorico de Chicago — 3006 N. Pulaski Road

Just a few buildings over from St. Joseph Village, the Ballet Folklorico de Chicago occupies a modest storefront at 3006 North Pulaski Road. Though architecturally unimpressive, it exemplifies Avondale's evolving mixed community. The organization was established in 2018 by three Latino professionals — previously the space housed a mobile phone store — with a mission to provide expert Mexican folklorico dance instruction for children and adults.

![Ballet Folklorico de Chicago building and performance]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_06_ballet_folklorico.png)
{:.image}
Ballet Folklorico de Chicago at 3006 N. Pulaski Road and a performance image (Ballet Folklorico de Chicago / Zillow, 2025)
{:.caption}

The organization is now the largest non-profit Mexican Ballet Folklorico institution in the entire Midwest, serving over 400 students across Chicago and neighboring communities and partnering with Chicago Public Schools for after-school programs. It has performed at MLB, NBA, and WNBA games. Adjacent to the century-old Polish Catholic institution at Stop 4, the Ballet Folklorico de Chicago represents a newer phase of institutional formation — creating an anchor with Mexican and Latinx roots in the same stretch of Belmont Avenue. It is a modern counterpart to the earlier immigrant institutions of the neighborhood, preserving dance, music, language, and intergenerational relationships for the Latino community.

#### Stop 6: Avondale Bowl — 3118 N. Milwaukee Avenue

![Avondale Bowl interior]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_07_avondale_bowl.png)
{:.image}
Interior of Avondale Bowl after renovation — the vintage eight-lane bowling alley now operates as a bar and event space (Interior Design / Studio 6F, 2022)
{:.caption}

Avondale Bowl is a great example of adaptive reuse success. The building is a vintage eight-lane bowling alley that had sat shuttered along Milwaukee Avenue for years. Luke Blahnik, who would lead its rehabilitation, first spotted a "for rent" sign in the window. Rather than demolish or radically remodel the building, the developers saw a unique opportunity to completely revamp the space while maintaining a piece of Avondale's social past. The result is a bar and event space that preserves the original bowling lanes and aesthetic.

Milwaukee Avenue has historically been a commercial and social corridor where immigrants gathered, "intermingled through local taverns and shops," like the original Northwest Bowl (Accidentally Wes Anderson). Avondale Bowl exemplifies preservation of leisure, community, and social fabric. This example of preservation and revitalization draws on a broader theme of reinvestment with respect for history: saving a quirky piece of the past can yield modern cultural and economic benefits.

#### Stop 7: Royal Discount Center — 3004 N. Milwaukee Avenue

![Royal Discount Center storefronts]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_08_royal_discount.png)
{:.image}
Royal Discount Center at 3004 N. Milwaukee Avenue — a longtime discount retailer serving diverse communities (Google Street View, 2025)
{:.caption}

The Royal Discount Center, though visually modest, is a critical example of how immigrant small businesses serve as cultural anchors across waves of ethnic succession. Previously operating under a different identity, the store has evolved to serve Avondale's current diverse population. The trilingual signage reflects its adaptation — an example of a business that maintains its original economic function while shifting its cultural service. Although not architecturally stunning, it represents a type of institution commonly overlooked in preservation discourse but central to neighborhood life.

#### Stop 8: Kurowski Sausage Shop & Rich's Bakery — 2976 N. Milwaukee Avenue

![Kurowski Sausage Shop exterior and interior]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_09_kurowski.png)
{:.image}
Kurowski Sausage Shop & Rich's Bakery at 2976 N. Milwaukee Avenue — exterior, signage, and produce interior (GoToPlace / BartGroceryBBQ, 2025)
{:.caption}

Kurowski Sausage Shop and Rich's Bakery was established in 1997. It should not be confused with the original Kurowski Sausage, founded in 1945 at 2900 North Milwaukee Avenue, which closed in the early 2000s due to changing neighborhood demographics, rising costs, and challenges facing small family businesses. That closure had a significant cultural impact: a 2003 Chicago Tribune article documented that 68% of respondents described the loss as "personally significant to cultural identity," while only 12% viewed it as merely a commercial change.

The current Kurowski is a brick storefront with housing above — typical of the mixed-use structures filling the Milwaukee Avenue corridor. The store sells traditional Polish foods including baked goods, sausages, and paczki. What makes it a significant neighborhood site goes beyond its food: it functions as a community gathering point, a place of warmth where "a regular or a first-time guest" finds a sense of belonging. The persistence of the Kurowski name and its Polish food traditions in a neighborhood that has shifted significantly demographically illustrates how ethnic businesses can survive and adapt across waves of succession.

#### Stop 9: St. Hyacinth Basilica — 3636 W. Wolfram Street

St. Hyacinth Basilica is arguably the most renowned location in the area. The parish had humble beginnings with 40 Polish families in 1894 in a small building on Milwaukee and Central Park Avenues. The current church was constructed from 1917 to 1921 in a Renaissance/Baroque Revival style by architects Worthman and Steinach — a Polish Cathedral featuring a three-towered facade, rare in American church architecture. The massive copper dome covers over 3,000 square feet with more than 150 figures. The interior contains more than 175 relics of saints, ornate stained-glass windows, and beautiful murals.

![St. Hyacinth Basilica exterior and interior]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_10_st_hyacinth.png)
{:.image}
St. Hyacinth Basilica exterior and interior — a Polish Renaissance/Baroque Revival church at 3636 W. Wolfram Street (Chicago Catholic / St. Hyacinth Basilica, 2024)
{:.caption}

The building's completion was delayed by financial and construction difficulties. Bronze front doors were installed in 2005. The church has served as a destination for influential people and politicians. Despite its deep Polish roots, the basilica has worked to make the Latino community feel welcomed, offering services in English, Polish, and Spanish. In 2023, Block Club Chicago reported that the basilica was seeking community support to fund ongoing preservation of its historic fabric — a reminder that iconic preservation is not a one-time achievement but a continuous effort.

#### Stop 10: Kosciuszko Park Fieldhouse ("Koz Park") — 2732 N. Avers Avenue

The final stop is the Kosciuszko Park Fieldhouse, fondly regarded by locals as "Koz Park." The park and fieldhouse are located at 2732 North Avers Avenue. The fieldhouse was constructed in a Tudor Revival style by architects Albert A. Schwartz and Frederick W. Bowes and dedicated in 1916, named after Polish hero Thadeuz Kosciuszko, who led an uprising against Russia in 1794. The fieldhouse expanded in 1936 to include an assembly hall, and the Chicago Park District added a natatorium in the 1980s. The park is 9.01 acres — significantly larger than Avondale Park — and was added to the National Register of Historic Places on October 16th, 2013.

![Kosciuszko Park exterior and detail]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandi_11_kosciuszko.png)
{:.image}
Kosciuszko Park Fieldhouse, 2732 N. Avers Avenue (Chicago Beautiful / Flickr/zol87, 2015)
{:.caption}

The park offers an indoor pool, gymnasium/assembly hall with stage, club rooms, baseball and softball fields, a football/soccer field, tennis courts, an artificial-turf soccer field, and a soft-surface ADA-accessible playground. It runs Special Olympics training, swimming programs, social clubs, and family museum excursions. As a Beautiful Chicago article notes, in an age of gentrification in the Northwest of Chicago, "individuals of diverse backgrounds, such as Latin America and Poland, have settled in the area and Kosciuszko Park continues to be a place where the community can congregate. It is a spot for recreation, live performances, and even outdoor movie screenings." The preservation of a shared space, evolving with changes in the community, is a defining example of the positive impacts of historic preservation.

---

#### Conclusion

Overall, Avondale along today's stretch of Milwaukee and Belmont is best understood as a landscape where each generation has built upon the last. The first two stops demonstrate how the city invested in basic infrastructure for newly arrived Polish and European immigrants in the early 20th century. The middle stops show how this industrial powerhouse developed, faltered, and was later reimagined: Shoemaker Lofts, Avondale Bowl, and St. Joseph Village are all examples of preservation not by freezing buildings in time, but by layering new uses while maintaining the importance of the past.

The commercial and cultural stops foreground a different continuity — the persistence of immigrant small businesses and institutions across waves of ethnic succession. The Royal Discount Center, Kurowski, and St. Hyacinth all have deep Polish roots but now operate in a neighborhood where Latinx residents are a large share of the population. Ballet Folklorico de Chicago represents a newer phase of institutional formation with Mexican and Latinx roots. Together, these sites show that Avondale's current mixed identity was not a clean break from the past, but the outcome of decades of overlapping immigration, shared spaces, and coexistence. Avondale's heritage lies not only in landmarked or renowned sites, but in churches, delis, schools, and parks that have quietly absorbed more than a century of change.
