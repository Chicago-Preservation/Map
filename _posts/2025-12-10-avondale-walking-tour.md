This walking tour explores how Avondale's built environment embodies over a century of immigration, labor, faith, and demographic change on Chicago's Northwest Side. The tour is centered along North Milwaukee and West Belmont Avenues. Through it, Avondale's historical arc is traced from an early 1900s manufacturing mecca settled by Polish and other European immigrants, through its mid-century development as a thriving working-class area anchored by Catholic parishes, into late 20th century Latinx migration and industrial decline, and into its 21st century state as a mixed-use area still maintaining much of its fascinating historical background.

The most important theme is immigration and cultural identity — focusing on how churches and ethnic establishments anchored their community through each wave of development. A second key theme is everyday architecture: factories, two-flats, and humble bungalows as symbols of the social fabric of working-class communities. The tour also highlights themes of urban change and resilience through how each generation adapts existing spaces and organizations to modern use. Both "exceptional" and ordinary architecture — ranging from a deli to a fieldhouse to a grand basilica — serve as living evidence of Avondale's historical and evolving social fabric.

## Stop 1: Avondale Park Fieldhouse
**3516 W. School Street**

![Avondale Park Fieldhouse, a neoclassical/colonial revival brick building at 3516 West School Street]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_01_park.png)

The park was opened in 1930 and its brick fieldhouse, designed by architect Clarence Hatzfield, was built in a neoclassical/colonial revival style by the Irving Park District. The land was acquired in 1925, and the park joined the Chicago Park District in 1934 when the Depression caused independent park organizations to consolidate. Originally five acres, the park lost its northeast portion — including the playfield and tennis courts — when the Kennedy Expressway was built in the 1950s. The park adapted, adding basketball and volleyball courts, a swimming pool, and a new playground in the early 1990s. A major $2.86 million renovation in 2020 modernized the historic fieldhouse and improved accessibility. Today it is one of only nine Chicago Park District gymnastics centers and serves as a neighborhood anchor providing green space and community identity.

## Stop 2: Frank W. Rielly School
**3650 W. School Street**

![Frank W. Rielly School, a solid brick school building on West School Street]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_02_reillyschool.jpeg)

Just down the street from the Avondale Park Fieldhouse, the Frank W. Rielly School is historic, with photos dating back to 1935. Between these two sites the classic two-flats and bungalows that line School Street represent naturally affordable housing — pre-1950s buildings whose age has kept them accessible to working-class residents. Established in the early 20th century, the school primarily served Polish and other European immigrant families working in adjacent brickyards and factories. As the area experienced a large influx of Latinx immigrants, the school's mission shifted with its demographics: it now has a 93% Latinx population, predominantly low-income. In December 1999, Rielly became the first Chicago Public School to install solar electric panels, with a computer in the front hall displaying real-time energy generation. This is an example of everyday preservation — a building maintaining its original purpose and role despite vastly changing demographics.

## Stop 3: Former Florsheim Shoe Company Building (Shoemaker Lofts)
**3963 W. Belmont Avenue**

![Former Florsheim Shoe Co. Building, now Shoemaker Lofts, a large multi-story brick industrial structure on West Belmont Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_03_flora.png)
![Former Florsheim Shoe Co. Building, now Shoemaker Lofts, a large multi-story brick industrial structure on West Belmont Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_03_florb.png)

The Florsheim Shoe Company building, now known as Shoemaker Lofts, received Chicago City Council landmark status on March 29, 2006. Built from 1924 to 1926 and designed by architect Alfred S. Alschuler, the building was constructed near the North Branch of the river and rail lines for easy import of raw materials and shipment of finished goods. At its height Florsheim employed 2,500 workers at this site, one of 71 retail outlets and 9,000 dealers. The building served as a crucial job-creating institution for Avondale's working-class immigrant population. When Florsheim scaled back Chicago operations in the late 20th century, the building sat vacant. In the early 2000s developers converted it into Shoemaker Lofts, a residential condominium — a successful adaptive reuse that preserved the exterior fabric and structure while paying homage to the building's original use in its name.

## Stop 4: St. Joseph Village of Chicago
**3525 N. Elston Avenue**

![St. Joseph Village of Chicago, a modern brick retirement community building on North Elston Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_04_stjosepha.png)
![St. Joseph Village of Chicago, a modern brick retirement community building on North Elston Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_04_stjosephb.png)

The current St. Joseph Village of Chicago, established in 2006, continues the mission of the Franciscan Sisters of Chicago, the first religious congregation founded in the city of Chicago. The congregation was established in 1894 by Josephine Dudzik — later Sister Mary Theresa — a Polish immigrant who began housing abandoned and less fortunate people in her home during the economic downturn of the early 1890s. In 1898 the congregation established the St. Joseph Home for the Aged and Crippled, which was the first and oldest nursing home in Chicago's history. The institution supported the community in multiple ways — operating sewing and vestment workshops, and growing flowers for cemetery bouquets. The current building preserves the bravery and kindness of the Franciscan Sisters and carries on their tradition of serving the elderly community in Avondale.

## Stop 5: Ballet Folklórico de Chicago
**3006 N. Pulaski Road**

![Ballet Folklórico de Chicago storefront on North Pulaski Road]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_05_balleta.png)
![Ballet Folklórico de Chicago storefront on North Pulaski Road]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_05_balletb.png)

Adjacent to St. Joseph Village, Ballet Folklórico de Chicago was established in 2018 by three Latino professionals — previously home to an "Up Mobile" store — with a mission to celebrate and share Mexican heritage, traditions, and dance. Founded to address the lack of cultural and artistic programs for Mexican families in the area, it is now the largest non-profit Mexican Ballet Folklórico institution in the entire Midwest, serving over 400 students across Chicago and neighboring communities. It partners with Chicago Public Schools for afterschool programs. Though architecturally unimpressive, this building is an important piece of community social fabric and a modern counterpart to the neighborhood institutions deeply rooted in Polish culture and immigration, anchoring Mexican cultural expression in the 21st century.

## Stop 6: Avondale Bowl
**3118 N. Milwaukee Avenue**

![Avondale Bowl interior, restored vintage 8-lane bowling alley with exposed brick and green theme]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_06_bowl.png)

Avondale Bowl is an example of successful adaptive reuse. The building is a vintage 8-lane bowling alley and bar that originally opened in the 1920s as Northwest Bowl and operated until the early 1990s. From the early 1990s it sat vacant until 2018, when new owner Luke Blahnik discovered the abandoned bowling alley in the building's second-floor storage space and seized the opportunity to preserve it. The name Avondale Bowl was chosen to demonstrate that this space belongs to the community. The original pinsetters and lanes were restored, and the green theme and exposed brick from the original walls were kept. The building is a symbol of the long-lasting immigrant and working-class history of the neighborhood, and the developers' commitment to preservation while creating a vibrant and popular bar shows that saving a quirky piece of the past can yield modern cultural and economic benefits.

## Stop 7: Royal Discount Center
**3004 N. Milwaukee Avenue**

![Royal Discount Center, a two-story brick storefront on North Milwaukee Avenue with signage in English, Spanish, and Polish]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_07_royaldiscounta.png)
![Royal Discount Center, a two-story brick storefront on North Milwaukee Avenue with signage in English, Spanish, and Polish]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_07_royaldiscountb.png)

The Royal Discount Center was originally called Syrena — a full-service Polish department store with newspaper ads dating back to 1982. Its Polish roots fit perfectly into Avondale's history as a Polish mecca in the Chicago area. Syrena was later incorporated as Royal Discount Center, operating since 1996. The store is an ideal example of local retail as a window into past economies and social exchange, providing affordable clothing and goods for the working-class population for decades. Today the store's signage is in English, Spanish, and Polish — a fascinating example of ethnic succession of a commercial space, demonstrating how a business with deep roots in one immigrant community can survive by adapting and gradually shifting to serve a new community while its physical location and primary function remain the same.

## Stop 8: Kurowski Sausage Shop & Rich's Bakery
**2976 N. Milwaukee Avenue**

![Kurowski Sausage Shop & Rich's Bakery, a brick mixed-use storefront on North Milwaukee Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_08_kurowski.png)

This stop illustrates both the loss and persistence of Polish cultural identity in Avondale. The original Kurowski Sausage, founded in 1945 at 2900 North Milwaukee Avenue, closed in the early 2000s due to changing demographics, rising costs, and challenges facing small family businesses. A 2003 Chicago Tribune article found that 68% of respondents described the closure as "personally significant to cultural identity." Around the same time, Kurowski Sausage Shop and Rich's Bakery opened in 1997 at this location on Milwaukee Avenue. The store sells traditional Polish foods — sausages, baked goods, paczki — from a modest brick storefront with housing above, a typical mixed-use structure along Avondale's main commercial corridor. With changing demographics, a Polish shop's ability to survive almost thirty years reflects a welcoming sense of community that extends to patrons across ethnic backgrounds.

## Stop 9: St. Hyacinth Basilica
**3636 W. Wolfram Street**

![St. Hyacinth Basilica, a massive Renaissance/Baroque Revival Catholic church with three towers, red brick and terra cotta, on West Wolfram Street]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_09_hyacintha.png)
![St. Hyacinth Basilica, a massive Renaissance/Baroque Revival Catholic church with three towers, red brick and terra cotta, on West Wolfram Street]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_09_hyacinthb.png)

St. Hyacinth Basilica is arguably the most significant institutional landmark in Avondale. The parish was established in 1894 by 40 Polish families; the current church was constructed from 1917 to 1921, designed by Worthman and Steinach in a Renaissance/Baroque Revival style. It features a rare three-towered facade, red brick clad with terra-cotta trim, large arched window bays, bronze front doors installed in 2005, and an interior with over 175 relics of saints, ornate stained-glass windows, and a massive 3,000-square-foot saucer dome with over 150 figures. In 2003 Pope John Paul II granted it the title of Minor Basilica, making it one of three in the Chicago area. The church maintains its Polish roots through Polish-language masses while also offering services in Spanish and English, reflecting the neighborhood's evolving demographics. Its tower restoration in 2023 cost $120,000, largely from the church's own savings — a testament to the challenges of maintaining these historic structures.

## Stop 10: Kosciuszko Park Fieldhouse
**2732 N. Avers Avenue**

![Kosciuszko Park Fieldhouse, a Tudor Revival brick fieldhouse at 2732 North Avers Avenue]({{site.baseurl}}/images/azghandiaurian_10_kosciuszko.png)

The final stop, fondly called "Koz Park" by locals, was dedicated in 1916 and named after Polish national hero Thadeuz Kosciuszko, who led an uprising against Russia in 1794. The fieldhouse was constructed in a Tudor Revival style by architects Albert A. Schwartz and Frederick W. Bowes. At 9.01 acres, it is far larger than Avondale Park, offering an indoor pool, gymnasium with stage, club rooms, four baseball/softball fields, a soccer field, tennis court, and an ADA-accessible playground. The fieldhouse expanded in 1936 and added a natatorium in the 1980s. The park was once home to one of two Polish-language Saturday schools in Chicago, and it was added to the National Register of Historic Places on October 16, 2013. Like so many stops on this tour, Kosciuszko Park is a premier example of social and material evidence of cultural life that has evolved over time as the population shifted — today a place where individuals of diverse backgrounds from Latin America and Poland continue to congregate for recreation, live performances, and outdoor movie screenings.

---

*Avondale's heritage is exceptionally rich despite not being a historically recognized district. Its churches, factories, schools, and storefronts collectively tell the story of every generation that has settled here — Polish, Latinx, and beyond — and demonstrate how ordinary architecture, not just celebrated landmarks, serves as living evidence of a neighborhood's evolving social fabric and enduring sense of place.*
